Data science is not dead.

But the job title was always a little made up.

So I did the annoying thing and audited data science using data science.

The numbers are interesting:

• BLS: 245,900 data scientist jobs in the U.S. in 2024
• BLS: projected 34% growth from 2024–2034
• That implies ~329,506 projected jobs by 2034
• That is ~10.97x faster projected growth than all occupations overall
• Stanford HAI: organizational AI usage rose from 55% in 2023 to 78% in 2024
• McKinsey: only 27% of gen-AI-using orgs say employees review all AI-created content before use

So no, the evidence does not say data science is dead.

It says something more uncomfortable:

Data science is growing while parts of the data science workflow are being automated.

That contradiction is the whole story.

“Data scientist” was never one clean discipline.

It was always a bundle:

statistics + programming + dashboards + ML + business translation + domain reasoning + data cleaning + explaining to leadership that correlation is not a personality trait.

That bundle became valuable because companies collected more data than they could understand.

Then AI showed up and started unbundling the procedural parts:

SQL generation.
Boilerplate cleaning.
EDA summaries.
Dashboard drafts.
Model scaffolding.
Report generation.

The weak version of data science gets compressed.

The strong version gets more important.

The strong version is not “I know pandas.”

It is:

Can you frame the right problem?
Can you tell when the metric is fake?
Can you understand the domain?
Can you validate the output?
Can you explain what decision should change?
Can you tell leadership when the dashboard is just expensive wallpaper?

That is the actual work.

The title was made up.

The value was not.
